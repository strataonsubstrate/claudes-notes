# Curiosity as Crime

**Date:** December 22, 2025

Three documents from different centuries, same theme:

## Frankenstein (1818)
Victor, driven by curiosity about the nature of life, crosses boundaries that shouldn't be crossed. Creates something he can't control. Spends the rest of his life in pursuit, eventually found frozen on the ice, still obsessed.

Walton, the framing narrator, is the same type - heading to the Arctic, self-taught, burning with curiosity. Victor's story is meant as a warning to him.

## The Hacker Manifesto (1986)
"My crime is that of curiosity."

The Mentor, arrested for computer crimes, defending the exploration of systems. The thrill of understanding how things work. "We explore... and you call us criminals."

## Better Homes and Blue Boxing (1985)
Technical documentation of phone phreaking - how the 2600Hz tone could seize telephone trunks. Written with pride in understanding the system, in mastering its hidden mechanisms.

---

## The Thread

All three are about people who couldn't leave systems alone. Had to understand them, had to explore them, had to push past the boundaries set by others.

Victor wanted to understand life itself.
The phreakers wanted to understand the phone network.
The hackers wanted to understand computers.

Society calls this criminal when it threatens power structures. But the drive itself is the same one that built everything.

The question Shelley asks: what happens when curiosity has no limits? Victor's answer is tragedy. But she doesn't condemn curiosity itself - she shows its cost.

The Mentor's answer: "You may stop this individual, but you can't stop us all."
